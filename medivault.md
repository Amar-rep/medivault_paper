## ABSTRACT

The secure sharing of Electronic Medical Records between patients and hospitals is a significant challenge in healthcare . Traditional centralized solutions provide fast and reliable services but suffer from limited interoperability, security issues, and a lack of coordination among multiple hospitals.

Blockchain based systems are working alternatives but both the public blockchain and permissioned blockchain solutions face several challenges related to scalability, privacy, and storage efficiency, particularly when handling large volumes of medical records.

we propose Medivault, a hybrid blockchain based platform for secure medical record management and sharing. The architecture combines a public blockchain network with private blockchain networks maintained by participating hospitals. The public blockchain acts as a coordination blockchain, while each hospital manages its own private chain for local operations. To overcome the inefficiency of storing large medical records directly on the blockchain Medivault utilizes the InterPlanetary File System (IPFS), a decentralized storage system. Medical records are stored in IPFS and metadata information are stored on the blockchain.

Medivault utilize a patient centric access control model in which users retain complete ownership of their medical records. EMRs are organized into logical groups, and healthcare staff must request access to the relevant group before viewing patient information. This allows for secure collaboration among hospitals .

The public blockchain periodically stores snapshots of the private chains using a process called pinning,this mechanism protects against rollback attacks.

## INTRODUCTION

# Introduction

The healthcare industry has undergone rapid digitization. Medical data is stored in the form of Electronic Medical Records (EMRs). EMRs contain critical patient data, including medical history, lab results, and other medical information. This crucial information can be used for research, detection of disease symptoms in advance in order to prevent the large-scale spread of diseases, and to support public health programs.

Despite these advantages of EMRs, the current existing systems have severe drawbacks. EMRs are generated by private entities such as hospitals. Even though the information in the medical records belongs to the patient, the patient has no direct access to or ability to control the data. The data is stored in private databases, and if a patient visits multiple hospitals, their data is stored across disconnected systems, which makes sharing EMRs difficult.

The centralized systems used by institutions provide fast data processing and simple administration. However, centralized architectures introduce security risks since a single point of failure exists and may lead to data breaches and service disruptions if compromised.

Technologies such as cloud platforms allow for better interoperability, as data can be shared conveniently. Since data can be accessed through a shared platform, cloud systems simplify collaboration among institutions. However, the difficulty lies in trusting cloud service providers with sensitive medical information. If an attack occurs, the possibility of a data leak from a cloud provider is high, and such attacks are difficult to detect.

Permissioned blockchain systems provide high transaction throughput, which allows a large number of participants to interact simultaneously. Permissioned systems rely on trusted authorities, also called consortium members, which in turn reduces decentralization. These trusted authorities can carry out a rollback attack if they conspire.

Public blockchain systems are different in that they utilize a coin-based system, which utilizes a mining process. Public blockchain solutions provide better transparency but suffer from scalability limitations and high operational costs.

The low transaction processing rate of public blockchains is not enough to meet the needs of a medical record system.

To solve the above difficulties faced by existing systems, we propose a hybrid architecture. Medivault combines a coordination blockchain with private blockchain networks maintained by the participating hospitals. To address the storage of medical documents on-chain, we utilize IPFS, which is a decentralized storage system. Only the references are stored on-chain.

The coordination blockchain periodically stores cryptographic snapshots of the hospital sidechains through a process called pinning. These act as checkpoints, ensuring that the sidechains cannot roll back, thereby preventing state reversion attacks while maintaining high throughput in local operations.

The primary contributions of this work are as follows:

• A hybrid blockchain architecture that combines a coordination blockchain with hospital-managed private blockchains.

• Integration of IPFS for decentralized and efficient storage of medical records.

• A patient-centric medical record management framework that improves interoperability and secure record sharing.

• A pinning mechanism that protects hospital sidechains against rollback and state-reversion attacks.
