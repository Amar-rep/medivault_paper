## ABSTRACT

The secure sharing of Electronic Medical Records between patients and hospitals is a significant challenge in healthcare . Traditional centralized solutions provide fast and reliable services but suffer from limited interoperability, security issues, and a lack of coordination among multiple hospitals.

Blockchain based systems are working alternatives but both the public blockchain and permissioned blockchain solutions face several challenges related to scalability, privacy, and storage efficiency, particularly when handling large volumes of medical records.

we propose Medivault, a hybrid blockchain based platform for secure medical record management and sharing. The architecture combines a public blockchain network with private blockchain networks maintained by participating hospitals. The public blockchain acts as a coordination blockchain, while each hospital manages its own private chain for local operations. To overcome the inefficiency of storing large medical records directly on the blockchain Medivault utilizes the InterPlanetary File System (IPFS), a decentralized storage system. Medical records are stored in IPFS and metadata information are stored on the blockchain.

Medivault utilize a patient centric access control model in which users retain complete ownership of their medical records. EMRs are organized into logical groups, and healthcare staff must request access to the relevant group before viewing patient information. This allows for secure collaboration among hospitals .

The public blockchain periodically stores snapshots of the private chains using a process called pinning,this mechanism protects against rollback attacks.

## INTRODUCTION

# Introduction

This paper analyses the utility of hybrid architecture for EMR(Electronic medical record ) management.The healthcare industry has undergone rapid digitization in recent years, resulting in the widespread adoption of Electronic Medical Records . EMRs contain critical patient information including medical history, laboratory reports,diagnoses, and treatment records. The availability of such data creates significant opportunities for medical research early disease detection and creating public health programs utilizing the information gatherd from medical records . EMRs act as a complete record of a patients medical history,Despite these advantages current existing systems have severe drawbacks. EMRs are generated by private entities like a hospital even though the information in the medical record belong to the patient , the patient has no direct access or ability to control the data .The data is stored in private database ,if the patient visits multiple hospital his data is stored accross disconnected systems which make sharing EMRs difficult.

Majority of the healthcare institutions rely on centralized private database systems for storing and managing medical records. These centralized systems provide fast data processing and administration but they introduce significant security risks. Since all information is maintained within a central infrastructure, a single point of failure exists that may lead to service disruptions, unauthorized access, or large scale data breaches if the centralized systems is compromised. Technologies such as cloud platoforms allow for better interoperability as data can be shared conviniently.Since data can be accessed through a shared platform cloud systems simply collaboration amoung institutions , however the difficulty lies with trusting cloud service providers with sensitive medical information. If an attack occurs the possibility of a data leak from a cloud provider is high and they are difficult to detect .

Public blockchain systems, on the other hand, provide greater transparency and stronger immutability guarantees through decentralized consensus mechanisms. These systems are typically supported by coin-based incentive models and mining processes. While public blockchains offer improved openness and resistance to tampering, they suffer from scalability limitations, high operational costs, and low transaction throughput. Furthermore, storing large medical documents directly on-chain significantly increases storage overhead and reduces overall system efficiency. The transaction processing capacity of existing public blockchains is insufficient to satisfy the demands of large-scale medical record management systems.

To address the limitations of existing solutions, we propose Medivault, a hybrid blockchain-based platform for secure medical record management and sharing. Medivault combines a coordination blockchain with private blockchain networks maintained by participating hospitals. The coordination blockchain facilitates interoperability and communication among institutions, while private hospital blockchains provide efficient local operations and record management.

To overcome the inefficiency of storing large medical documents on the blockchain, Medivault utilizes the InterPlanetary File System (IPFS), a decentralized content-addressable storage network. Medical records are stored within IPFS, while only cryptographic references and access-control metadata are maintained on the blockchain. This approach significantly reduces blockchain storage requirements while preserving data integrity.

Additionally, the coordination blockchain periodically stores cryptographic snapshots of hospital sidechains through a process known as pinning. These snapshots act as verifiable checkpoints, preventing sidechains from rolling back their state and mitigating state-reversion attacks while maintaining high throughput for local operations.

The primary contributions of this work are as follows:

• A hybrid blockchain architecture that combines a coordination blockchain with hospital-managed private blockchains.

• Integration of IPFS for decentralized and efficient storage of medical records.

• A patient-centric medical record management framework that improves interoperability and secure record sharing.

• A pinning mechanism that protects hospital sidechains against rollback and state-reversion attacks.
